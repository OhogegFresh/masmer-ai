Concept 01
Cap table
Short for "capitalization table." It's just a spreadsheet that tracks who owns what percentage of the company at any given moment. Every founder, investor, and employee option gets a row.
Concept 02
Dilution
When new shares are created for an investor, everyone's percentage goes down — even though they still own the same number of shares. That's dilution. Your slice gets smaller, but the pie itself gets bigger.
Concept 03
Pre-money valuation
What everyone agrees the company is worth before the investment arrives. If a company is valued at $4M pre-money and raises $350K, the post-money valuation is $4.35M. This number determines the investor's ownership %.

Founders

Define who owns the company before any investment or option pool. Shares are just a unit — the percentage is what actually matters.

Why 10 million shares? It's just a convention. You could use 100 or 100 billion — the percentages work the same. Using a large number like 10M makes future math cleaner when you divide shares among employees, advisors, and investors without ending up with fractions.

2

Option pool

Shares reserved for future employees, advisors, and key hires. Investors require this before funding, and it comes out of your equity — not theirs.

The option pool shuffle. Here's the part most founders miss: the option pool is carved out before the investment is calculated. That means it dilutes you, not the investor. A 10% option pool on a 10M share company means 1M shares are set aside — and your percentage shrinks before the investor even shows up. Standard range is 10–15%.

3

Investment round

When you raise money, new shares are created and issued to the investor. The pre-money valuation and investment amount together determine how many new shares to create — and what percentage the investor ends up with.

How new shares are calculated: New investor shares = (Investment ÷ Pre-money valuation) × existing shares. So if pre-money is $4M and you raise $350K, the investor gets 8.75% of the company. The formula creates exactly enough new shares to represent that ownership percentage.

The investor would own more than 30% — this is high for a pre-seed round. Consider raising your pre-money valuation or reducing the investment ask.

What each stake is worth on paper

Post-money valuation = pre-money + investment. Multiply each person's percentage by the post-money value to get their paper value. This is not cash — it only becomes real money when the company is sold, goes public, or does a secondary sale.

6

Vesting — protecting the cap table

Equity should be earned over time. Vesting prevents a co-founder or early employee from leaving after 3 months and keeping their full ownership forever.

Standard schedule: 4 years with a 1-year cliff. The "cliff" means nothing vests in the first 12 months — if someone leaves before month 12, they get zero. At month 12 they get 25% all at once. Then the remaining 75% vests gradually each month over the next 3 years. Always vest co-founders. Always vest yourself.

Why this matters

Without vesting, a co-founder who leaves after 3 months keeps their full ownership percentage — forever. That means they could own 20% of a $10M company ($2M) for 3 months of work.

With a 4yr/1yr cliff, if they leave at month 3 they get zero shares. Those shares return to the company or option pool.

Investors also check vesting before writing a check. Unprotected equity is a red flag — it means someone could hold the company hostage.
