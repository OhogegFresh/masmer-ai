Jobber

Origin & Funding

Founded in 2011 by Sam Pillar and Forrest Zeisler in Edmonton, Alberta. Pillar had worked as a freelance software developer and business analyst before starting the company. Jobber has stayed disciplined about fundraising — Pillar has publicly said the company avoided raising during the 2021 valuation bubble because it wasn't a business need, and frames every round around having "a true path to profitability and massive market opportunity" rather than chasing valuation.

Total funding sits between $191M and $237M depending on the tracker (Tracxn vs. CB Insights), raised across 6–7 rounds from investors including Point Nine, Summit Partners, OMERS, and General Atlantic. The most recent disclosed round was a $61M growth equity raise in March 2024.

Business & Revenue

2024 revenue reached $167.5M, up from $143.2M in 2023. As of May 2026, Jobber has surpassed 100,000 customers and facilitated over $100 billion in services processed through the platform. The company doesn't disclose profitability publicly, but its funding discipline and Pillar's public comments suggest it's run with profitability as a real constraint, not just a talking point.

Marketing & Product Direction

Marketing is founder-led and deliberately human — Pillar has said their approach has "stayed the same since 2011, speaking to the very human side of running a home service business." Beyond brand storytelling, Jobber runs quarterly Home Service Economic Reports and a "Blue-Collar Report" addressing the trades labor shortage, positioning itself as an industry voice, not just a vendor.

Most relevant to Masmer: Jobber has already shipped an AI receptionist. Its AI Receptionist reached general availability in August 2025 and has handled over 200,000 conversations across participating businesses. It also launched "Jobber Voice" in September 2025, hands-free voice control for 100+ tasks inside the mobile app. Jobber is not waiting on AI features — it's already in market with the exact wedge Masmer is building around.

ServiceTitan

Origin & Funding

Founded in 2007 by Ara Mahdessian and Vahe Kuzoyan, two Stanford-educated software engineers who built the first version to help their immigrant fathers' plumbing and contracting businesses. The company launched its cloud platform in 2012 and grew through Mucker Capital's seed funding before raising a total of roughly $1.4 billion across 8 rounds from Bessemer, ICONIQ Growth, Battery Ventures, TPG, and Index Ventures.

ServiceTitan IPO'd in December 2024, pricing at $71/share and raising approximately $625M, with shares opening at $101 and the company valuing near $8.9 billion on day one.

Business & Revenue

FY2025 revenue closed at $771.9M, up 26% year-over-year, with roughly 9,500 active customers (up 18%) and $68.5B in gross transaction volume processed through the platform. The company posted a GAAP net loss of $239.1M for the year but a non-GAAP net income of $16.5M — meaning it only became profitable on an adjusted basis after 17+ years and $1.4B+ in capital, and is still GAAP-unprofitable. As of June 2026, market cap sits around $7B on roughly $1B in trailing revenue.

Why This Matters for Masmer

ServiceTitan is the enterprise benchmark, not a direct SMB threat — its ~9,500 customers (versus Jobber's 100,000+) shows it serves far fewer, much larger accounts. This is useful for market-sizing slides but the wrong company to compare unit economics against. The contrast worth highlighting to investors: even a company this well-funded took nearly two decades to turn adjusted-profitable, which makes Masmer's smaller, leaner, paid-from-day-one model a deliberate contrast, not a compromise.

Hearth

Origin & Funding

Founded in 2016 in Austin, Texas, Hearth is fintech-first — its core product lets contractors offer customers monthly financing instead of lump-sum project costs, layered with quoting, invoicing, and client management tools. Total funding is reported inconsistently across sources: Forge cites ~$110M, PitchBook cites $61.8M, Tracxn cites $39.1M. Investors include 8VC, Founders Fund, Human Capital, and SuRo Capital. Its last disclosed round was a $23M Series B in May 2021.

Business & Revenue

Hearth claims over 20,000 home improvement professionals on its platform and more than $500M in jobs funded through its financing product. But one revenue-estimate provider puts annual revenue at just $6.7M against an estimated $21.5M valuation — a steep markdown relative to the capital raised. This is the clearest signal in the set that raising money and building a financing-led product doesn't automatically translate into proportional revenue growth.

Why This Matters for Masmer

Hearth is a useful caution, not a template. It's more a financing layer wrapped around light CRM features than a true operations platform — APRs on its loans range from 5% to nearly 36%, which puts a chunk of its margin and trust exposure inside consumer lending, not software. If anything, Hearth shows that "we help contractors get paid" alone isn't a defensible enough wedge without a stronger core CRM and AI layer behind it — which is exactly where Masmer's three-system loop differentiates.

Workiz

Origin & Funding

Founded in 2015 by Saar Kohanovitch, Idan Kadosh, and Erez Marom — two of whom worked as locksmiths for over 15 years in San Diego and built Workiz after getting frustrated with pen, paper, and spreadsheets to run their own business. This is the most direct "built by an operator" parallel to Masmer's own positioning in this set.

Workiz raised $60M total across 4 rounds from Aleph, Lead Edge Capital, New Era Capital Partners, and Magenta Venture Partners — starting with a $5M Series A in 2019 and a $13M Series B in 2021.

Business & Revenue

By 2024, Workiz reported $29.8M in revenue with 100,000 customers and 141 employees. Pricing runs from $225/month for 3 users (Kickstart plan) up to $1,675+/month for 15 users with advanced features, and add-on modules cost an extra $200–$300/month each — real, premium-leaning pricing for an SMB tool, not a race-to-the-bottom freemium model.

Marketing & Product Direction

Workiz's signature feature is the Workiz Service Phone System — automated call recording, ad-source tracking, and call masking — built on the founders' insight that "all service calls start with a phone call." That's functionally the same problem Masmer's AI receptionist is solving, just without the AI layer. It's the most direct stage-and-shape comp for Masmer: similar founding story, similar revenue-per-customer (~$300/customer/year vs. Jobber's ~$1,675/customer/year — likely reflecting cheaper entry tiers and a smaller average account size), and a Series A that's a more realistic funding benchmark for Masmer's stage than Jobber or ServiceTitan.

What This Actually Means for Masmer
1

AI receptionists are not a blue ocean anymore

Jobber's AI Receptionist hit general availability in August 2025 with 200,000+ conversations handled, and Workiz already owns the "phone call is where every job starts" insight with its Service Phone System. Masmer's wedge needs to be sharper than feature parity — likely pricing, simplicity, or vertical depth, not "we also have an AI receptionist."

2

Workiz is the realistic funding benchmark, not Jobber or ServiceTitan

Workiz's $5M Series A on an early working product with real revenue is a far more comparable stage to Masmer's pre-seed raise than ServiceTitan's $1.4B trajectory or Jobber's decade-plus of disciplined growth. Use Workiz's early numbers in investor conversations, not the giants.

3

"Built by a contractor" is a crowded narrative, not a unique one

ServiceTitan, Workiz, and arguably Jobber all tell some version of "founder lived the problem." This positioning earns credibility but doesn't differentiate against this set — it differentiates against generic enterprise tools. The pitch needs a second layer beyond origin story.

4

Paid-only pricing matches the market's actual behavior, not just investor signaling

Workiz charges $225–$1,675+/month plus paid add-ons and still has 100,000 customers. Contractors already pay real money for tools that save them time. Masmer's $99–$349 tiers sit comfortably inside what this market has proven it will pay — this isn't just a story for investors, it's supported by a live comp.

5

Profitability takes a long time at this revenue model, even for the best-funded player

ServiceTitan needed 17+ years and $1.4B to become non-GAAP profitable, and is still GAAP-unprofitable. This is a useful data point for investor conversations about realistic timelines — and a reason to keep burn low and lean on the "paid from day one" model rather than assuming scale alone fixes margins.

6

Capital raised doesn't guarantee revenue — Hearth proves it

Hearth raised tens of millions but one estimate puts its revenue at just $6.7M. Capital efficiency and clear product-market fit matter more than total raised. Worth referencing if an investor pushes Masmer to raise more than the round actually needs.

The gap to fill isn't AI receptionists. It's the loop.

Every comp here has at least one strong piece — Jobber's brand and AI rollout, ServiceTitan's enterprise depth, Hearth's financing, Workiz's phone system. None of them tie receptionist, CRM, and AI estimating into one connected loop built specifically for the contractor who can't afford four separate tools. That's still Masmer's opening.
